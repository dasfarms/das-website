Hydrangea arborescens

A little about parents of Annabelle - Hydrangea arborescens, or smooth hydrangea, is a native species with habitat from New York to Iowa and south to Florida. This plant can be grown in full sun or full shade. Full sun placement may require additional water in hot climates. The inflorescence of this parent is a flat corymb of almost all non showy flowerlets. It is mainly found in moist soils under a hardwood forest canopy and is often common along woodland road banks and streams. The leaves of smooth hydrangea are large (8 to 18 cm long), and deciduous. The stem bark has a peculiar tendency to peel off in several successive thin layers with different colors, hence the common name "sevenbark". Smooth hydrangea can spread rapidly by stolons to form colonies.

Annabelle

A little about Annabelle - Annabelle is a stunning white hydrangea, often producing heads over 10 in. in diameter. Unlike the better known blue and pink hydrangeas (macrophyllas), Annabelle blooms every year even after severe pruning or intensely cold winters. The huge, white drumstick blooms appear in profusion without fail. This spectacular hydrangea is the one we recommend for customers in colder climates since it flowers on the current year's growth. Cut to the ground at the end of winter, and it can still achieve a height of six feet and bear masses of huge rounded heads of white flowers up to ten inches across. Uncut the shrub can reach ten feet or more, but the flower heads tend to be smaller. Some people plant 'Annabelle' as a hedge, cutting it back severely in the winter for a tidy effect. This is definitly a superior plant introduced by J. C. McDaniels of the University of Illinois.

Characteristics

Zone 3-9, Height: to 5', Spread: to 5', Growth: fast, Bloom: white, Bloom Time: early to mid June

How to grow Annablle

- This plant is very fibrous rooted and transplants easily. Make sure you have good drainage and make a hole about 12 inches in diameter and depth. If soil is poor mix in some conditioner, a couple double handfuls of peat moss, pine bark fines, or other organic matter should be sufficient. When you purchase Annabelle you are purshasing the root system as you are going to cut the top growth to within 4 inches of the ground in late fall or early spring. Very adaptable as to soil type and PH. May require a good watering in hot dry weather. You will have no problem growing this wonderful plant, adding a little fertilizer in the spring would be nice.

Plant Early

Early spring is a good time to plant as long as the ground is not frozen. In early spring, roots begin new growth and continue to develop at a fast rate, and a little later top growth begins. While the same plant planted in late spring gets a slow start due root adjustment, the early-planted plants are becoming well established. When summer finally arrives, the early-planted plant is far better equipped to deal with heat and drought, largely due to its well-established root system.
